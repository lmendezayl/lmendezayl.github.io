[19/03/2025] On getting behind

Hello internet.

 

Today is the first day of my fifth semester at college. But I'm still not proud of who I've become academically over the past two and a half years. According to the number of courses I've passed, I should be in my third semester—yet here I am, embarking on a new chapter, one I fear deeply.

 

The subjects I'm taking this semester range from easy to extremely hard, with Advanced Analysis claiming the title of hardest. That subject alone changed the way I see math. Sadly, I'm retaking it because I failed last year. Long nights were spent crying over it, the crushing weight of inadequacy wrapping around my thoughts, even driving me to question whether this path was meant for me—whether choosing Data Science as my major was a mistake.

 

But I got over it. Shit happens, and it will keep happening. They say these moments are what make us stronger.

 

Yet another feeling grew deeper than self-doubt—one that doesn’t just affect me, but everything around me. The gnawing sensation that everyone else is moving forward while I’m stuck at the starting line. The fear of falling behind in life, in academia. The dread that every short-term decision I make might ruin my future, while others take risks and leap ahead.

 

This feeling isn’t new. It’s been here since I left high school—the constant whisper that I’m not doing enough, being enough, while others reap the rewards of their hard work. (Though in reality, we only see what others want us to see—more on that later.)

 

At 18, I started Systems Engineering at UTN, but I was a different person back then. I didn’t believe in college, so I didn’t try.

 

At 19, a brief switch to Computer Science made me realize coding was for me—even though I barely knew how to write a single line. Meanwhile, some of my high school classmates were already in their sophomore year. I tried not to dwell on it, but my self-esteem was at rock bottom—breakups, family issues, financial instability. To me, none of that mattered. I was behind because of my own failures: bad choices, lack of intelligence.

 

Then, at 20, I met two guys in my Algebra 1 class (shoutout to Aaron y Kevin, lqm). They made me realize Computer Science wasn’t my calling—I wanted to be a mathematician. Naively, I didn’t realize that wasn’t for me either.

 

Two and a half weeks into pure math—Linear Algebra and pre-Advanced Calculus—I knew I loved math, but not that kind. I wanted applied math. A friend in Data Science (thanks, Tiago) helped me navigate the syllabus for Computational Linear Algebra, the subject I’d switch to if I abandoned pure math. So I did. I switched.

 

By then, I knew I was behind. But it truly hit me at 21. While I was still figuring out what I wanted, some people were already working. Some had graduated. My own girlfriend had. Some even lived on their own. Friends I made in college—people who started with me—were now a semester or two ahead. While I was stuck switching majors, they were moving forward with their lives.

 

Maybe they don’t doubt themselves. Maybe they don’t feel that suffocating pressure every time they make a life-altering decision.

 

But the truth? They do. Everyone does. Every person who’s ever achieved something great has, at some point, hit rock bottom.

 

And here I am—still climbing.
